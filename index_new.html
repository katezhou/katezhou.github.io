Bio

I research and design human-LM interactions to bridge the gap between experimental systems and real-world applications. Situated at the intersection of natural language processing and human-computer interactions, my work marks a new era of AI where natural language acts as the primary interface to empower human-AI collaboration. My recent work focuses on three key stages of the human-LM interaction design pipeline: 1) model linguistic overconfidence from training data[EMNLP'23] 2) human feedback that exacerbates model miscalibrations [ACL'24] , and 3) the impact of linguistic overconfidence on human reliance[ArXiv'24. My research often falls at the intersections of Natural Language Processing, Human-Computer Interactions, and Computational Social Science.

I'm an sixth-year Ph.D. student in Computer Science at Stanford University, advised by Dan Jurafsky. Prior to coming to Stanford, I served on the University of Washington Board of Regents as appointed by Governor Jay Inslee. I got my B.Sc., B.Se., M.S., from the University of Washington where I studied Computer Science and Human Centered Design and Engineering where I won the School of Engineering Dean's Medal of Excellence. Throughout my graduate studies, I spent several summers doing internships at Microsoft Research (FATE) and Allen Institute for AI (Mosaic).
